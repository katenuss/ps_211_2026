PS 211: Introduction to Experimental Design

Fall 2026 · Section C1 · Instructor: Kate Nussenbaum

COURSE DESCRIPTION

This course is an introduction to the logic and processes involved in descriptive and inferential statistics for psychology. Topics include frequencies, measures of central tendency and variability, statistical inference, significance, t-tests, ANOVAs, correlation, regression, chi-square tests, and statistical software analysis.

PREREQUISITE

General Psychology (PS 101). The course will make use of both the programming language R and the interface RStudio, but no prior programming experience or knowledge of R is expected.

COURSE OBJECTIVES

By the end of this course, students should understand:

  • Different types of variables and how to measure them.
  • The difference between populations and samples, and the basic concept of sampling.
  • Different types of descriptive and inferential statistics common in psychological research.
  • The concept of hypothesis-testing.
  • How to use R to conduct basic statistical analyses of data.
  • How to use R to create visualizations of data.
  • How to report and interpret the results of inferential statistical tests.

Exams: 3 × 15% + 1 × 25% = 70%

Data Write-Up: 2 × 10% = 20%

  • Apply statistics learned in class to analyze a dataset in R.
  • Write a formal report including research question, hypotheses, methods, and results (with figures) in APA format.
  • Data write-ups will be largely completed in groups during discussion section.
  • Late assignments lose 3 percentage points each day they are late. No late assignments will be accepted more than 1 week past the deadline.

Discussion Participation: 10%

  • Actively participate in discussion conversations and exercises.
  • Work in small groups to complete worksheets and data write-ups.
  • There are 13 discussion sections throughout the semester. They will be graded on a pass/fail basis. You need to pass 10 to earn full credit for your discussion participation grade. If you show up, actively participate, and engage with in-class exercises, you will “pass” that section.

  • If you miss a discussion section, contact the TF and/or your classmates to find out what you missed. You can also check the course website so that you can complete any handouts on your own.
